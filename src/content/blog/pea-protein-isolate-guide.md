Pea protein isolate has become one of the most versatile plant-based protein sources in the supplement industry. Derived from yellow split peas (*Pisum sativum*), it offers a clean amino acid profile, excellent solubility, and broad consumer appeal for vegan, allergen-free, and clean-label products.

## Amino Acid Profile

Pea protein isolate typically delivers 80–85% protein content with a strong concentration of branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs). While it's slightly lower in methionine compared to whey, it excels in arginine and lysine — making it a strong candidate for blends that pair with rice or hemp protein for a complete amino acid profile.

> For most RTM applications, a 70/30 pea-rice blend delivers an amino acid profile comparable to whey protein concentrate at a significantly lower cost basis.

## Sourcing Considerations

The global pea protein market is dominated by producers in Canada, France, and China. Canadian-origin pea protein commands a premium but offers consistency in quality and non-GMO verification. Chinese-origin material has improved significantly in quality but faces tariff headwinds under current Section 301 duties.

Key sourcing factors to evaluate include protein content (target ≥80%), mesh size for solubility, color (lighter is generally preferred), and taste profile — some sources carry a stronger legume note that requires more aggressive flavoring.

## Formulation Tips

- Use instantized grades for RTM applications — non-instantized will clump in shaker bottles
- Pair with natural flavors like chocolate, vanilla, or chai to mask the legume base note
- Consider adding a small percentage of MCT powder (2–3%) to improve mouthfeel
- pH adjustment with citric acid can improve flavor perception in fruit-flavored formulations

## Regulatory & Labeling

Pea protein isolate is classified as a food ingredient (not a dietary ingredient) and appears on labels as "Pea Protein Isolate" in the ingredient statement. For organic claims, the source material must be certified organic and the facility must hold organic processing certification.

Prop 65 exposure is generally low for pea protein, but testing for lead and cadmium is recommended — especially for California-distributed products. Safe harbor levels apply, and most quality pea protein sources test well below threshold.

### Bottom Line

Pea protein isolate remains one of the strongest plant protein options for contract manufacturing. The combination of clean taste, allergen-free status, competitive pricing, and consumer demand makes it a foundation ingredient for any plant-based product line.
